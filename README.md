Figure 1. The fireside niche is a dynamic light environment and social space. 
We quantify firelight with a SpectraScan PR-670 spectroradiometer (Photo Research, Syracuse, USA) at a distance of 1 m and height of 75 cm. We provide the raw Firelight data for flames and coals, as well as the calculated data for luminance used in panels B-D. In panel E we quantify firelight with a spectral irradiance colorimeter (OHSP-350S; range 350-950 nm; Hopoo Light & Color Technology, Hangzhou, China) at a distance of 1 meter and height of 75 cm, and provide the colour spectrum data. 

In panel (B) Variation in the luminance of flames and coals is plotted. Horizontal lines represent median values of 119 and 12 cd m-2 [2° standard observer] and were calculated based on 60 and 20 measurements, respectively, with a SpectraScan PR-670 spectroradiometer (Photo Research, Syracuse, USA; distance = 1 m, height = 75 cm). 

In panel (C) Variation in the emission spectra of flames and coals. Dark lines represent the median spectra; and vertical white lines represent ± 1 SD. The inset illustrates normalized absorbances of selected photopigments. 

In panel (D) we reproduced published data (blue circles) from laboratory studies (in humans without use of pupil dilators) investigating the effects of long exposures (> 2 h) to broadband light sources on melatonin suppression (Brown et al., 2022). We converted firelight spectra to Melanopic equivalent daylight illuminance (Melanopic EDI) lux values (red circles) with luox, an open-source web platform for calculating physiologically relevant quantities for light and lighting (Spitschan et al., 2022). The blue whiskers represent ± 1 SD; the dashed red lines represent median Melanopic EDI values of 1.8 (coals) and 30.4 (flames) lux; and approximate levels of melatonin suppression (file: Firelight data (firepit)_spectra-luminance).

In panel E, we quantify firelight spectral irradiance. The dominant wavelengths extend into the near infrared (NIR) range; i.e., thermal radiation that is invisible to human vision but detectable as heat (file: Firelight data_radiance_spectrum_Fig1E).

Figure 2. Firelight flicker rate quantified through high-speed video capture.

We quantified the flicker rate of firelight using the entire flame at 500 fps using a high-sensitivity colour camera (FASTCAM SA1.1; Phototron, Tokyo, Japan), with postprocessing of slow-motion video performed using custom software built in MatLab. We  provide the Flickerate data as separate RGB channels (file: FlickerRate data_Bonfire RGB Levels). In panel (A) Each frame was separated into RGB colour-space and the image intensity of each pixel quantified, weighted, and averaged. In panel (B) Frame intensity was plotted as a function of time in frequency space using a Fourier transform. The code available to support plotting these data is also provided.
